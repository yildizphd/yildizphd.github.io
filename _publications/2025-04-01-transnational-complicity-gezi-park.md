excerpt: 'This study examines how social movements get suppressed, with a particular focus on the role that liberal democracies play in enabling repression beyond their own borders. It identifies two ways this happens. Liberal democracies can lend legitimacy to repressive governments through symbolic or performative diplomacy, and they can provide material support that makes repression possible. This contributes to social movement theory by showing that repression extends beyond direct government action to involve the indirect complicity of liberal democracies. To explore these dynamics, the study focuses on the Gezi Park Movement, a major wave of activism in contemporary Türkiye, a hybrid regime that is characterized by features of both democracy and authoritarianism. Drawing on documentary evidence, survey data, and a protest event analysis, it traces how repression operated through both the hybrid regime environment and the involvement of liberal democracies. The study also draws lessons from the Gezi experience for other movements. These include anticipating repression that comes from unexpected or indirect sources and managing international attention carefully, as well as building strong internal coordination, setting realistic and incremental goals, and communicating with a clear and consistent message.'
